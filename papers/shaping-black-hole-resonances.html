Shaping black hole resonances I. Black hole ringdown as a spectral filtering process

Alejandro Svyatkovskyy Kholyavka, Jose Antonio León Vega, Samuel Gómez Gómez, Xisco Jiménez Forteza, Sayak Datta · 2026

This paper studies how a black hole responds when it is perturbed. The central idea is that black hole ringdown can be understood as a spectral filtering process: the final signal depends not only on the black hole, but also on the frequency content of the perturbation that excites it.

Main idea

After a black hole is disturbed, it emits a characteristic signal known as the ringdown. This signal is made of damped oscillations called quasinormal modes. Each mode has a complex frequency, whose real part controls the oscillation and whose imaginary part controls the damping.

The paper shows that the excitation of these modes is controlled by the overlap between the initial perturbation and the black hole's resonant response. In simple terms, the black hole reacts more strongly when the perturbation contains frequencies close to its natural quasinormal-mode frequencies.

$$ \Psi(t) \simeq \sum_n C_n e^{-i \omega_n t} + \Psi_{\mathrm{tail}}(t) $$

Here, $\omega_n$ are the quasinormal-mode frequencies and $C_n$ are the excitation amplitudes. The main question is how the initial data shape the coefficients $C_n$.

Spectral filtering picture

A useful way to interpret the result is to think of the black hole as a filter. The perturbation provides an input spectrum, and the black hole selects the parts of that spectrum that match its resonances.

$$ C_n \sim B_n \, T_n $$

In this schematic expression, $B_n$ represents the black hole response and $T_n$ represents the spectral content of the initial perturbation. A mode is strongly excited when both factors overlap efficiently.

Why it matters

This result clarifies how ringdown signals are produced. It shows that the observed strength of a quasinormal mode is not determined by the black hole alone: it also carries information about the perturbation that excited the black hole.

This is relevant for gravitational-wave astronomy, where ringdown signals are used to test general relativity and to probe the nature of astrophysical black holes.
