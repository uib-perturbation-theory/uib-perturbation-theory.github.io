New study explores how black holes “ring” after being disturbed

A new paper from the group studies how black holes respond when they are perturbed, and why some ringdown signals become stronger than others. When a black hole is disturbed, it does not return to silence immediately. Instead, it produces a characteristic signal called the ringdown. This signal is similar to the sound of a struck bell: it is made of specific frequencies known as quasinormal modes. These frequencies are important because they are directly linked to the properties of the black hole. In the paper “Shaping black hole resonances I. Black hole ringdown as a spectral filtering process”, the authors show that a black hole can be understood as a kind of resonant filter. This means that the black hole reacts more strongly when the perturbation contains frequencies close to its own natural frequencies. The work was submitted to arXiv on 23 May 2026 by Alejandro Svyatkovskyy Kholyavka, Jose Antonio León Vega, Samuel Gómez Gómez, Xisco Jiménez Forteza, and Sayak Datta. The main message is simple: the final ringdown signal depends not only on the black hole itself, but also on how the black hole is disturbed. If the initial perturbation is well tuned, some modes are strongly excited. If it is not, those modes can be much weaker. The study combines analytical calculations with numerical simulations. The authors show that the excitation of each mode is controlled by the frequency content of the initial perturbation. In other words, the black hole selects the parts of the perturbation that match its own characteristic frequencies. To test this idea, the paper also introduces QNMToolkit, a fitting method designed to extract ringdown modes from time-domain signals in a robust way. This helps quantify how reliable the extracted mode amplitudes are. This work provides a clearer way to understand black hole ringdown signals. It may also help future studies of gravitational waves, where ringdown observations are used to test general relativity and to learn more about the nature of black holes.
